# Go-to-Market — Content Engine

## 1. Market Context

The AI writing tool market has exploded over the past two years, and it's created a problem that didn't exist before: businesses are now producing more content than ever, but an increasing proportion of it sounds identical. The tools are the same (ChatGPT, Jasper, Copy.ai), the prompts are generic, the output is generic, and audiences are getting very good at recognising AI content precisely because it has no discernible voice.

This is the opening. Growing businesses — specifically those 5–50 person companies with a marketing function but no copywriting budget — are caught in a frustrating loop. They know they need content. They know AI can speed things up. But the output doesn't sound like them, so they spend as much time editing as they would have spent writing, and the content still doesn't perform the way professionally written copy does.

The timing is right for a different kind of AI content tool — one that starts with the client's brand, not with a blank prompt. The shift in the market from "AI can write" to "AI should write like *us*" is happening now. Early movers who position themselves as the expert-led, brand-first alternative to generic AI tools have a clear window to establish themselves before the larger players catch up (if they ever do — the agency relationship model creates a structural advantage that a SaaS subscription can't easily replicate).

For Lawrence specifically, this is a high-leverage moment. He has the methodology, the client relationships, and the credibility to position Content Engine as the reason to choose his agency over any other. Not just a copywriter — a copywriter who gives you a custom AI content system built on six years of professional expertise.

## 2. Launch Strategy

Content Engine doesn't need a public launch — at least not initially. The model is agency-first: Content Engine launches *to Lawrence's clients*, not to the world. This changes the entire GTM calculus. You're not trying to generate hundreds of sign-ups; you're trying to convert 3–5 existing or prospective agency clients within 90 days and prove the value of the platform as a retainer differentiator.

**Pre-launch (now → first client):** Build Content Engine to production readiness, complete the tone of voice workshop with one existing client, onboard them, and validate that the output quality justifies the promise. This is the proof-of-concept phase — everything else depends on it working.

**Soft launch (first client → 3 clients):** With one client successfully using the platform and producing content they're happy with, start using Content Engine as a sales tool in agency conversations. Show it in discovery calls, not just describe it. Add it explicitly to retainer proposals. Target 2–3 additional clients within 90 days of the first successful onboarding.

**Public visibility phase (month 4 onwards):** Once you have 3+ clients actively using the platform, you have proof points, case study material, and a refined pitch. This is when broader visibility — LinkedIn content, conversations in marketing communities, strategic outreach — makes sense. You have something real to show.

## 3. Pre-Launch Playbook

**Weeks -8 to -6: Build and validate**

The primary goal in this period is getting Content Engine to a point where it can be used by a real client without embarrassment. Ship Phase 0–3 of the roadmap. Use Lawrence's own copywriting framework as the default test document and generate content for hypothetical clients across all seven content types. Review every piece critically: does it meet the bar of "publishable with minor edits or none"? Iterate on the prompt architecture until it does. Do not rush this — one client with a bad first experience is harder to recover from than a delayed launch.

While building, start making small moves on LinkedIn. Not announcements — just presence. Share one post per week about something you've noticed in the content marketing space, something about what makes copy actually work, or an observation about the AI content problem. The goal is not to build a following; it's to warm up the algorithm and remind your existing network that you have strong opinions about content.

**Weeks -5 to -3: Client selection and preparation**

Identify the right first client. The ideal first client for Content Engine is an existing retainer client who: (1) trusts you enough to give honest feedback, (2) produces content regularly enough to use the platform weekly, and (3) has a clear enough brand voice that the tone of voice workshop will produce a usable document. Avoid choosing a client whose brand voice is very complex or in flux — keep the first deployment straightforward.

Run the tone of voice workshop with this client. The output of this workshop is the first real test of the entire system — the quality of the AI generation is only as good as the context documents. Produce the three markdown files (tone of voice, content guidelines, copywriting framework) and upload them to the staging environment. Generate test content and review it with the client before launch.

**Weeks -2 to -1: First client onboarding**

Complete production deployment. Set Lawrence's admin account. Finesse the invitation flow and test it end-to-end with a test email. Invite the first client, walk them through their first session (ideally on a screenshare), and gather structured feedback. Target: client generates at least 5 pieces of content and rates at least 3 as "ready to publish or nearly there." Fix any issues that emerge. Document the onboarding process — you'll repeat it.

## 4. Launch Week Plan

"Launch week" for Content Engine is the week you have your first client generating content consistently. This is not a public launch — it's an internal milestone — but it deserves structured attention.

**Monday:** Client generates their first piece of content. You're on standby to review and give feedback. Note any friction points in the UX, any output quality issues, any confusion about the interface. Your job today is observation, not fixing.

**Tuesday:** Fix the top 2–3 issues surfaced on Monday. These should be small — if they're significant, you have a larger problem to address before scaling. After fixing, check in with the client to see if they've generated more content. Ask: "How did it feel?" Listen for hesitation.

**Wednesday:** First content review. The client should have generated at least 3–5 pieces across different types. Review each one. Give feedback on the output quality as if you'd written it yourself — this is your framework, your voice guidelines, your output. If a particular content type is consistently missing the mark, iterate on the prompt architecture.

**Thursday:** Assuming output quality is solid, prepare to use Content Engine in your next agency sales conversation. Update your retainer deck to include a slide about "the Content Engine" — what it is, what it gives the client, why it's different from generic AI tools. Not a product pitch — a differentiator.

**Friday:** Review the week. Questions to ask yourself: (1) Is the client generating content they're happy with? (2) Is the output quality what you'd want to show a prospective client? (3) Are there any obvious UX issues to address before the second client? (4) What would make the system meaningfully better in the next two weeks?

## 5. Post-Launch Growth

**Weeks 1–4: Quality first, then clients**

The most important thing in the first month is not growing the number of clients — it's making the first client experience excellent. Monitor their usage: are they logging in weekly? Are they generating content? Are they actually publishing it? If usage drops off, reach out proactively. The risk in this period is not a bad review — it's silent disengagement. A client who stops logging in without telling you anything is more dangerous than one who gives you critical feedback.

Fix what needs fixing quickly. At this stage, changes to the prompt architecture, the form fields, or the UI have minimal risk — there's one client. Move fast.

**Weeks 5–8: Second and third clients**

With the first client stable and producing content, start using Content Engine in sales conversations in earnest. The pitch is simple: "As part of your retainer, you get access to a custom AI content tool built on your brand voice. I run a workshop with you to capture your tone of voice, content guidelines, and messaging framework. After that, you can generate on-brand content across seven types — blogs, social posts, email, website copy, case studies — without needing to prompt or edit heavily."

Target clients you're already in conversation with or existing clients whose retainers are up for renewal. You don't need a cold outreach strategy at this stage — warm conversations convert at a much higher rate, and you only need 2–3 more clients in this period.

After onboarding each client, run a retrospective: what worked in the tone of voice workshop? What's missing in the content guidelines? Where does the output fall short? This feedback loop directly improves the system for all subsequent clients.

**Weeks 9–12: Prove the model and prepare for scale**

By week 12, with 3–5 clients actively using the platform, you should have enough material to identify patterns: which content types are most used, which generate the best output, where clients get stuck, what they love. Write down these observations — they will inform the next round of product improvements and the public-facing story you'll start telling.

Start collecting proof points. Ask active clients for specific feedback: "Is there a piece of content you've generated and published that got a good response?" Even a single case study — "this LinkedIn post generated 3 enquiries" — is enormously powerful sales material. Document it.

If Content Engine is working, the natural next step is to offer it as a standalone value-add to prospects who haven't worked with you before — not as a SaaS product, but as part of a "Content Engine retainer" package with a clear scope and price point.

## 6. Channel Strategy

**1. Agency retainer conversations (highest ROI, immediate)**

The highest-return channel is the one Lawrence is already in: existing client relationships and active prospects. Content Engine doesn't need to generate inbound leads to succeed — it needs to be the reason Lawrence's retainer proposals are chosen over alternatives and renewed without hesitation. Every discovery call, every proposal conversation, every renewal meeting is an opportunity to demonstrate Content Engine. Show it; don't describe it. A live demo of a client generating on-brand content in under 5 minutes is more persuasive than any slide deck.

Expected effort: low (already happening). Expected return: high (directly drives 90-day goal). Timeline to results: immediate.

**2. LinkedIn (medium-term, compound)**

Lawrence's target clients — marketing people and founders at 5–50 person businesses — are on LinkedIn. Content Engine's pitch resonates there because the LinkedIn algorithm rewards content about productivity, AI, and business growth, and the target audience is exactly who Lawrence should be talking to. The approach: write posts in Lawrence's own voice about the content problem (not about Content Engine specifically). Topics: "why AI content sounds like everyone else", "what actually makes copy perform", "the tone of voice workshop process", "before/after examples" (anonymised). 2–3 posts per week, written with genuine opinion — no fluff, no generic takes. Tag clients (with permission) when a piece of their content gets traction.

Expected effort: medium (2–4 hours/week). Expected return: medium (builds awareness over 3–6 months). Timeline to results: 3+ months for meaningful inbound.

**3. Direct outreach to warm prospects (short-term, targeted)**

Once Content Engine is live with 1–2 clients, reach out directly to 5–10 prospects who have previously expressed interest in working with Lawrence but haven't converted. The message is simple: "I've just launched a new tool for my clients — thought you'd find it interesting. Happy to show you a demo." Don't lead with "AI writing tool" — lead with the problem ("tired of AI content that doesn't sound like you?") and the outcome ("we build your brand into the system so the first draft is already good"). Aim for a demo call, not a sign-up. Close from the demo.

Expected effort: low (targeted, not volume). Expected return: high (warm prospects convert at much better rates). Timeline to results: 30–60 days.

**4. Word of mouth via client success stories (medium-term, high trust)**

Happy clients who talk to their network are more valuable than any marketing channel. Make it easy for clients to talk about Content Engine by: (1) giving it a clear name and elevator pitch they can repeat ("I use a custom AI content tool that my agency built for my brand"), (2) producing content that gets noticed so clients have something to point to, and (3) asking explicitly at the 90-day mark: "Is there anyone in your network you think would benefit from this?" Referrals from existing clients are the warmest possible leads.

Expected effort: low (enabled by product quality). Expected return: very high (high trust, high conversion). Timeline to results: 3–6 months.

## 7. Content Strategy

Lawrence is a copywriter. His content marketing should be the proof of what he sells — it should be good. Not just regular, but demonstrably well-written, direct, and useful. This is a competitive advantage most agency owners ignore.

**What to create:**

*LinkedIn posts (3x/week):* Short, opinionated, first-person. Topics that resonate with the target audience and demonstrate expertise: the content consistency problem ("why does your content go quiet every time things get busy?"), the AI quality problem ("I tested 7 AI tools this week — here's what they all got wrong"), practical content advice ("the one question I ask every client before I write a word of copy"), behind-the-scenes of the tone of voice workshop process. Do not post about product features. Post about the problem and the worldview.

*LinkedIn articles (1x/month):* Longer-form, more substantial. These compound over time and attract people searching for content strategy advice. Topics: "Why your AI content sounds like everyone else (and what to do about it)", "How to capture your brand voice before you can't remember it", "Content consistency: the small business guide to never missing a week again."

*Case studies (as they emerge):* Once a client produces content that gets results, document it. Keep it specific: "Client X generated a case study using Content Engine. It was shared 47 times on LinkedIn and led to two new enquiries." Publish on LinkedIn and keep a running log for sales conversations.

**What not to create:**

Don't start a newsletter until you have at least 200 subscribers to justify the effort. Don't start a podcast. Don't create YouTube content. These channels take 6–12 months to produce meaningful returns and require more production effort than is justified at this stage.

## 8. Community Strategy

The target users — marketing managers and content-responsible people at growing businesses — gather in predictable places. The approach is participation, not broadcasting.

**LinkedIn groups and hashtags:** Not worth the effort for reach, but useful for identifying prospects. Search for posts tagged #contentmarketing, #smallbusiness, #marketingtips — these are people who are actively trying to solve the problem Content Engine addresses. Engage genuinely with the 1–2 posts per week that are most relevant: add a specific observation, not a sales pitch. Build a reputation as someone worth following before you ever mention Content Engine.

**Marketing Slack communities:** Communities like Online Geniuses, Marketers Chat, and similar spaces have active marketing-focused members. The approach: join, listen for 2–4 weeks, then participate in conversations about content, AI, and brand voice. Never pitch. Offer specific, useful perspectives. Build relationships with the moderators and most active members. These communities convert slowly but the relationships are high-value.

**Agency peer networks:** Lawrence's peers — other freelancers and small agency owners — are a valuable but different audience. Don't position Content Engine as something they should build too. If the tool ever becomes licensable, that's a future conversation. For now, peer network conversations are useful for learning what's working in the market and staying visible in the freelancer ecosystem.

**Direct engagement with target companies on LinkedIn:** Identify 20–30 companies in the 5–50 person range that fit the target profile and follow their pages. When their marketing person posts about content challenges, engage. Not to sell — to be useful. This builds familiarity before any sales conversation.

## 9. Key Metrics

The 90-day goal is 3–5 paying agency clients actively using Content Engine as part of their retainer. Everything tracks back to that.

**Primary metric:** Active client workspaces generating content weekly. Target: 3 by day 30 (first client), 5 by day 90.

**Acquisition metrics:** Number of demo conversations completed per month (target: 3–5). Conversion rate from demo to retainer with Content Engine included (target: >50% — if you're showing it in demos, it should close). Number of existing clients moved to Content Engine retainer (target: 2–3 by month 3).

**Activation metrics:** Time from workspace creation to first content generation (target: <7 days — if a client hasn't generated in a week, they need a nudge). Number of content pieces generated in the client's first session (target: 3+ — the magic moment lands on the first session or it risks not landing at all).

**Engagement metrics:** Content pieces generated per workspace per week (target: 3+, "excellent" = 5+). Content types used per workspace (target: 3+ types regularly — diversity of use indicates the platform is genuinely useful, not just tried once). Weekly active workspace rate at 90 days (target: 100% — these are paid retainer clients, not free users who can go dormant).

**Quality metrics (manual):** Lawrence's rating of output quality per client per month (% of pieces rated "publishable with minor edits or none" — target: 80%+ by month 2). Client-reported satisfaction at 30-day check-in (qualitative — note any hesitations or frustrations).

**Revenue proxy (no in-app payments):** Retainer value attributable to Content Engine per client. Not formally tracked in the app, but Lawrence should be able to point to at least 3 retainer renewals or upgrades directly influenced by Content Engine by month 6.

## 10. Budget Considerations

Content Engine's GTM is almost entirely zero-budget in the initial phases — which is appropriate given that the distribution strategy is relationship-driven, not paid-acquisition-driven.

**Infrastructure costs (unavoidable):**
- Convex Cloud, Vercel, Resend: ~$0–25/month combined at low scale
- Anthropic Claude API: ~$15–65/month depending on generation volume (10 clients, moderate use)
- Total infrastructure: **~$15–90/month** — trivially low given the retainer revenue this enables

**Optional investments worth considering:**
- Loom ($15/month): Record a short product demo video (3–5 minutes) that shows a client generating content end to end. This is more persuasive than a live demo in async sales conversations. Use it in follow-up emails after discovery calls.
- LinkedIn Premium ($40–70/month): Useful for InMail outreach to warm prospects and for seeing who's viewed your profile. Optional — only worth it if you're doing active outreach beyond your warm network.
- Notion or similar for client documentation ($10/month): Maintain a simple CRM — track which prospects you've demoed, what their objections were, what their timeline is. Free tools (Airtable, Notion free tier) work fine.

**What not to spend money on at this stage:**
Paid LinkedIn ads or any paid acquisition. The unit economics of paid acquisition only make sense when you have a scalable, repeatable sales motion — which requires at least 5–10 clients and a clear value proof. At 3–5 clients, invest time, not money, into the GTM.

**Total recommended monthly budget: £50–100** — mostly infrastructure and one optional tool (Loom or LinkedIn Premium). If you're spending more than this before hitting 5 clients, something is off.

## 11. Risks

**Risk 1 — First client experience underdelivers.** The entire GTM depends on the first client generating content they're proud of. If the output quality is poor or the UX is confusing, they'll use it twice and stop. This is the highest-impact risk because all subsequent client acquisition uses social proof from early clients. Mitigation: run an internal quality bar (Lawrence must be satisfied with the output quality across all 7 types before inviting any client), accompany the first client through their first session, and iterate quickly on any issues before the second client is onboarded.

**Risk 2 — Tone of voice workshop outputs don't translate to effective AI context.** The quality of Content Engine's output depends entirely on the quality of the context documents — and those documents come from a workshop process that hasn't yet been tested in this format. If the workshop outputs are too vague, too long, or structured in ways that confuse the AI, the output won't be meaningfully better than generic tools. Mitigation: develop a standard markdown template for each document type that's been tested for AI context quality before the first real client uses it. Test with multiple hypothetical clients before going live.

**Risk 3 — The premium of Content Engine doesn't justify the retainer increase.** If clients use the platform but don't see measurable improvements in their content performance, it becomes a nice-to-have rather than a must-have. This makes retainer renewals harder to justify. Mitigation: set up a lightweight tracking process from day one — ask clients at the 30-day check-in what content they've published and whether they've seen any response. Even anecdotal evidence ("this post got three times my normal engagement") is valuable for building the proof point.

**Risk 4 — Lawrence's admin overhead grows faster than expected.** As the client base grows, reviewing high-priority content, managing guideline documents, and monitoring workspaces could become a significant time commitment. If Content Engine creates more work than it saves, the model breaks. Mitigation: track Lawrence's time per client per month from the first onboarding. If admin time per client is growing rather than shrinking as the platform matures, address it with process changes (batching reviews, clearer client guidelines) before scaling.

**Risk 5 — Generic AI tools rapidly improve their brand voice capabilities.** ChatGPT, Jasper, and others are actively developing brand voice features. If they reach a point where their output is indistinguishable from Content Engine's, the technical differentiation weakens. Mitigation: the real moat is not the technology — it's the agency relationship, the tone of voice workshop, and Lawrence's personal involvement in high-priority content. These are structural advantages that a SaaS subscription can't replicate. Lean into them in all client conversations.

**Risk 6 — Slow sales cycle delays the 90-day goal.** Even warm prospects can take 4–8 weeks to move from conversation to signed retainer. With a 90-day target, there's limited time for slow-moving deals. Mitigation: prioritise clients who are already in conversation or whose retainers are up for renewal in the next 30–60 days. Offer Content Engine as an upgrade to an existing relationship rather than a reason to start a new one. New client acquisition takes longer than upselling.
